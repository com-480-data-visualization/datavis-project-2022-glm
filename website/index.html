It's Getting Greener

Over the last few centuries, the availability of energy has had a big impact on humanity. New energy sources have been discorvered, such as fossil fuels, nuclear and hydropower, but the quantity we produce and consume has also eveolved. Energy demands are growing which poses a threat to renewable energy sources since they are not exploited to the same extent as fossil fuels. How has the quantity of clean vs non-renewable energy consumed evolved over the last century?

Renewables and fossils comparison

We start by taking a look at six main sources of energy:
Gas
Uses natural gas in power plants to generate electricity through burning
Coal
Uses different types of coal in power plants to generate electricity through burning
Oil
Made from dead organisms buried underneath sedimentary rock and subjected to both prolonged heat and pressure
Solar
Uses sunlight to generate electrical energy through photovoltaic panels
Wind
Uses the kinetic energy created by air in motion to produce electricity
Hydro
Uses the natural flow of moving water to generate electricity
Gas, coal and oil are all non-renewable energy sources. They exist on Earth in a finite amount and cannot be replaced naturally at a pace quick enough to keep up with consumption. Eventually, these fossil-based resources will become too costly to harvest and humanity will need to shift its reliance to other sources of energy.

Three main replacements are solar, wind and hydro. These are called renewable energy sources because they are naturally replenished on a human timescale. Renewable energy often provides energy for electricity generation to a grid, air and water heating/cooling, and stand-alone power systems.

Energy consumption (TWh) in Europe since 1965

In Europe, energy consumption as a whole increased during the industrial growth following WWII, from the early sixties up until the early nineties. In contrast to the global picture, energy consumption has been stable in Europe from the nineties and onward.

This is likely due to the larger proportion of developed countries in the continent and the pressure that inhabitants put on their government to bet more on renewable sources of energy.

Renewables consumption in TWh by country

It is indeed getting greener

The animated map on the previous slide shows the evolution of the renewable energy consumption over the years. It can be noted that the intensity of the color is getting higher which indicates overall growth in renewables consumption. When approaching the 2000's, we see that China, the United States and Brazil are the "greenest", however, keep in mind that these countries also have a high population that consumes more energy.
When analysing countries individually, the respective charts show a clear growth over time, so more renewable energy is produced and consumed generally speaking. There are nontheless some countries like Algeria, Switzerland, etc. that present different behaviors. Algeria, for example, hits its lowest level in the years 2000-2002. This may be related to the Algerian Civil War which ended in 2002 as the the country's focus was channeled elswhere. Switzerland, on the other hand, displays a rather uniform behavior over the years. This might be an indicatior of the country's contstant concern about climate change and getting clean energy.
